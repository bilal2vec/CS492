Introduction

CAPTCHAs have become a common security measure to protect websites from automated bots. However, their implementation often poses challenges for people with disabilities, leading to accessibility issues. This demo will explore the difficulties of CAPTCHAs and suggest alternative solutions that prioritize both security and accessibility.

What are Captchas

CAPTCHAs (Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart) are a type of challenge-response test used to determine whether the user is human or an automated bot. They are commonly employed on websites to prevent spam, automated submissions, or abuse from bots. There are various types of CAPTCHAs, including:

  • Text-based CAPTCHAs: These require the user to identify and type a series of distorted letters and/or numbers displayed in an image. The distortions make it difficult for bots to recognize the characters.
  • Image-based CAPTCHAs: Users are asked to identify specific objects within a set of images, such as selecting all images that contain a particular object (e.g., traffic lights, buses, or bicycles).
  • Audio CAPTCHAs: Users listen to an audio clip with distorted speech or background noise and type the words they hear. This type of CAPTCHA is primarily intended for visually impaired users.
  • Mathematical CAPTCHAs: Users solve a simple math problem, like adding or subtracting two numbers, and enter the answer to prove they are human.
  • Google's reCAPTCHA: This system utilizes advanced risk analysis techniques to determine whether a user is human or a bot. It often requires users to click a checkbox that says "I'm not a robot" and, depending on the user's behavior, may present additional challenges like image recognition tasks.
CAPTCHAs help protect websites from spam and malicious activities but can also create accessibility challenges for users with disabilities, such as those with visual impairments or cognitive difficulties.
